For each positive integer $k$, let $S_k$ be the set of real numbers that can be expressed in the form
$$\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}+\dots+\frac{1}{n_k}$$
where $n_1,n_2,\dots,n_k$ are positive integers.\\
Prove that $S_k$ does not contain an infinite strictly increasing sequence.\\

\underline{\textbf{Solution}}

We prove by induction on $k$. When $k=1$, the statement is obviously true.\\
For the sake of contradiction, assume $S_{k-1}$ does not contain an infinite strictly increasing sequence, while $S_k$ does.\\
Let the infinite strictly increasing sequence be $a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots$.\\
Let $n_{i,j}$ be such that $a_j=\sum_{i=1}^{k}\frac{1}{n_{i,j}}$ for all $j\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}$, with $n_{i,j}\leq n_{i+1,j}$ for all $1\leq i\leq k-1$ and $j\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}$.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\underline{Claim:}, leftmargin=*]
\item
$n_{1,j}$ can only take on finitely many values.

\item[\underline{Proof:}]
Assume the contrary. Then there exist a $j$ with $n_{1,j}>n_{k,1}$. So $n_{1,1}\leq n_{2,1}\leq\dots\leq n_{k,1}<n_{1,j}\leq n_{2,j}\leq\dots\leq n_{k,j}$.\\
Therefore $$\frac{1}{n_{1,j}},\frac{1}{n_{2,j}},\dots,\frac{1}{n_{k,j}}<\frac{1}{n_{1,1}},\frac{1}{n_{2,1}},\dots,\frac{1}{n_{k,1}},$$
which means $a_j<a_1$, contradicting its minimality.

\end{enumerate}

Now we can partition $a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots$ into finitely many strictly increasing sequences based on their value of $n_{1,j}$. For each of them with a fixed $n_{1,j}$, subtracting $\frac{1}{n_{1,j}}$ from each term turns it into a sequence in $S_{k-1}$, and we can conclude each of the strictly increasing sequences are finite from the inductive hypothesis. Therefore $a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots$ is a finite sequence as well and we arrive at a contradiction, proving the statement for all $k\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}$.
